  4. Scheduled weekly Lab which either can be solo or with one other person. Here is my youcanbookme link. You can also message me on Slack or email me to set up a time.

  5. Choose a partner in your class (or two) who you can code with over video chat or reach out to in order to ask questions when you're stuck. Have one person from you buddy group Slack me privately to tell me who your partner(s) is/are.

  6. Your personal website and journal are be up-to-date and PUSHED every week by Friday and here is how you do that in your terminal in case you forget:

    • cd ./desktop/repositories/____.github.io
    • git add .
    • git commit -m "message of what you did"
    • git push

  7. You write a journal entry every week showing:
    • what you worked on that week
    • why it matters to you
    • how you're feeling about it
    • the progress you made
    • the frustrations you encountered
    • what you learned
    • what your next steps are


  8. We'll be having some fun conversations about issues relevant to computer science. I expect you to complete the assigned reading/podcast before class

Here are some guidelines about process for the items on this checklist

  • Coming up with an idea: you have an authentic need (like a gift you want to give someone) OR you're inspired by something you see perhaps on the class website or on Jennifer Dewalt's website of code sketches.
  • Planning: You make a quick blueprint or mock up of what you're going to make and how it's going to work (here is my octopus arm code sketch and mock up.)
  • You program it: You use sublime text (or another text editor), and you have it open next to your Chrome browser window. Each time you make a change, you save and refresh your browser window. You use the console to debug. You google things when you don't have the words for them (e.g. how do I set the width for a div CSS) and look at W3 and MDN. You refer to the class resources page for videos and cheat sheets. When you're stuck, you message the class slack channel and we'll help you!

Download this PDF and read this article from the New Yorker called Dressing for the Surveillance Age and then make a sketch of a design for a clothing item, mask, or any kind of product that, based on what you read, you think will make the wearer invisible or will be adversarial to the systems that are identifying us. Feel free to use the paper app on a phone to draw or do it by hand. Take a photograph of your drawing and post it on your personal website with a description so that other people will be able to see it. Put the link to your sketch in this google doc.

Consider the following questions as you read:
  • Based on what you've read, what are some applications of computer vision that you find exciting? What are some that you are concerned about?
  • What does it mean that he gets an "invisibility cloak". In what ways does it make him invisible.
  • What is adversarial fashion? What is it's purpose and why does it work? What's the goal of it? Check out some adversarial fashion here.
  • What do you think a neural net is?
  • Do you believe that you should have a right to privacy and anonymity? Is there a cost to this right?
 What not to do:
You skim the reading and don't highlight (in preview you can highlight) parts that you find interesting. You were multitasking while reading so you can't recall an examples of adversarial fashion. For your drawing you made something that one of the people referenced in the article has already made.

What to do:
read the questions in the first column and keep them in your mind. annotate your article on your computer, if you don't print it out, so that when we talk about it, you can reference ideas you had while reading. Make sure to mark things that you find particularly interesting! Do a Google serch up terms you don't know.
